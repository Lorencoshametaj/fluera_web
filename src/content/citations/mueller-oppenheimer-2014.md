Three experiments. Students watched TED talks and took notes either by hand or on a laptop. Laptop note-takers produced longer, more verbatim notes. Handwriters produced shorter, more paraphrased notes. Thirty minutes or a week later, both groups were tested. Handwriters significantly outperformed laptop-takers on conceptual questions. On factual recall, performance was similar.

The authors' interpretation: the slowness of handwriting forces cognitive selection and compression — the very acts that drive deep encoding. Laptop transcription can substitute typing speed for thinking.
